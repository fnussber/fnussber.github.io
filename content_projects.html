Astronomical Observations Queue Visualization

Gemini Observatory
Developer, Analyst
2014
Scala Java Swing

The optimal execution time for observations that get selected for execution during a semester depends on many parameters like the visibility and altitude of the target in the sky, weather conditions, additional time constraints, moon phase, availability of instruments and specific instrument configurations and many more.

Gemini's Queue Visualization Tool (QV) supports the planning team in the long- and mid-term scheduling of observations by providing a wide variety of filter options to select subsets of all observations in the observations database and then sort them by some parameters (e.g. remaining hours) and visualize the visibility and altitude above horizon.

In addition to filter, group and sorting functionality QV also allows to turn on or off a number of constraints like instrument availability or minimum elevation and sky brightness (given by the moon phase) to allow for a "what-if" analysis.

While originally planned for some specific use cases, the final application turned out to be flexible enough to cover a wide variety of useful "query" operations on the database and is now used in contexts outside of mid- and long-term queue planning, too.

Clearance Support for High Powered Laser Usage in Astronomy

Gemini Observatory
Developer, Analyst
2013
Java Vaadin Spring Hibernate Jetty

Astronomical observatories often use high powered lasers to produce artifical "guide stars" (i.e. points of light) on high atmospheric layers. These are used as a reference in Adaptive Optics systems in order to correct for atmospheric turbulences. Although highly unlikely, the use of such lasers could interfere with the operation of known (and secret) satellites in space and has therefore to be cleared by the US military.

Aim of this project was to replace a labor intense manual process with an automated one and also to improve the support for the telsecope operators during the night. The new process automatically collects for each night of when the laser is used potential observation targets for that night from the observatory's observations database depending on their visibility and other criterias and compiles a target list which is uploaded to a dedicated web site run by the military. Once the target list has been processed the system is notified by email and will then download the clearance list which defines for each target of that night at which times down to the second it is allowed to propagate the laser.

To make sure these clearance windows are adhered to the application is constantly monitoring the current telescope pointing position and comparing it with those windows. If it detects that the laser is used outside a window that has been cleared by the military the laser is automatically shuttered.

A web frontend allows to see the current state of he system and gives visual and audible clues when during long during observations a closure window is approaching and the laser needs to be shuttered.

Network-wide Track Management System for Swiss Federal Railways

Ergon
Swiss Federal Railways
Team Lead Business Layer, Developer
2007 - 2008
Java WebSphere Maven

The Swiss Federal Railways (SBB) operate one of the densest railway networks in the world. In order to replace an existing planning software with a more modern solution with improved functionality a team of 25 developers and UI specialists from SBB and external companies was tasked during two years with a complete rewrite based on an Eclipse RCP consuming services provided by a WebSphere infrastructure backed by an Oracle database. Other aspects covered by the business layer are the daily train schedules, journey time calculations based on actual train parameters like engines and weight and checks which can be used to validate if planned schedules are feasible.

I was responsible to orchestrate the development effort of the 5 head strong Business Layer Team and also actively involved in the development, planning decisions and some architectural decisions. Main focus of the Business Layer Team was to provide services like for example detailed information about rolling stock and train compositions or railway network information including detailed track information down to traffic lights and signalisation.

After an earlier attempt to replace the existing software had failed because of the complexity of this project, SBB wanted to try an agile development process. Great care was also taken to involve a group of end users in the development process. This continuous user feedback on the current state of the software proved invaluable to verify the requirements and to detect and address usability and performance issues at an early stage.

For more information also see a product prospectus on the web page of another external company that was involved in the project.
